You are the senior software engineer at a start-up company developing an exciting new mobile app that will allow salespeople to generate and email sales quotes and customer invoices from their smartphones. You were given stock options when you joined the company, and if it has a successful initial public offering of stock, you will be able to sell these options for at least $10 million.​

Your company's sales force has led a major corporation to believe your product will be available next week.​

Unfortunately, at this point the software still contains quite a few bugs. The leader of the testing group has reported that all of the known bugs appear to be minor, but it will take another month of testing for his team to be confident the product contains no catastrophic errors.​

Because of the fierce competition in the mobile app industry, it is critical that your company be “first to market." To the best of your knowledge, a well-established company will release a similar product in a few weeks. If its product appears first, your start-up company will probably go out of business.​

In each of your answers below, make sure that you show us that you understand the theory. 

  1. Analyze the dilemma using act utilitarianism.
  2. Analyze the dilemma using rule utilitarianism.
  3. Analyze the dilemma using deontology (Kantianism)
  4. Analyze the dilemma using virtue ethics.
Your Answer:

1. Act utilitarianism prioritizes the actions which result in the most overall happiness. For instance, in this scenario, you could evaluate ether or not to take the time to fix the bugs by weighing the associated costs and benefits from the scenario. If you were to take the time to fix the bugs, then the users of the mobile app may have a more satisfactory experience, but as was stated earlier in the prompt, there would be significantly fewer users that this would apply to. Furthermore, all of the associated developers of the company would suffer greatly, not simply because of the minor inconvenience of the app, but because they would lose their jobs if the company were to shut down. Furthermore, you would lose out on the $10 million stock options when the company would IPO. On the other hand, if you did not take the time to fix the bugs immediately, and pushed the software with the bugs, then there would only be minor inconveniences from dissatisfied users. So there is overall more happiness if you push the software now with the bugs.

2. Rule utilitarianism prioritizes the rules that, when universally applied, lead to the most overall happiness. If all companies and developers were to push their software with bugs, then no products would ever work, which would lead to everyone being dissatisfed with everything. As such, you should not push your code with bugs, because if everyone did this, then the overall happiness would be less.

3. Deontology states that rightful actions are those that adhere to the set of given written rules. As such, since the ACM code of ethics that computing professionals should hold themselves to advocates for the proper development of their code without bugs. So according to that you should fix the bugs. Furthermore, the first categorical imperative of Kantianism states that we should all treat others the way that we would want to be treated. The second categorical imperative of Kantianism states that we should treat everyone as a means, not simply as a means to an end. So, according to these, if we were receiving software, we would want it to work, so we should take the time to fix the bugs.

4. Virtue ethics states that it is not only our actions that matter but also the emotions and intent behind our actions, our inherent desire to act a certain way. While acting out of the characteristics of integrity, it makes sense that we would want to not deliver faulty software when advertising that it works to the customers. So, we should fix the bugs so that we are not lying about our product's capabilities.

1a. 10/10, correct definition and full elaboration/analysis for the examination of various courses of actions, such as the benefits and harm. 1b. 7/10: Defines rule utilitarianism properly, but doesn't select a universal rule. In this particular scenario, honesty is the best policy. The response should be shaped around that. 1c. 10/10, good definition and application of the various imperatives of Kantianism 1d. 10/10Good definition from an individual perspective for virtue ethics.

According to Nancy Leveson, what causal factors contributed to the Therac-25 accidents? Present four factors and explain each one.

Your Answer:

Overconfidence in Software: With this causal factor, developers of the Therac-25 placed too much confidence and reliance that their software would properly work. In this case, when developers become confident in their software they may not fully conduct a thorough review on the operationality of the software. This was the case here, as when the developers believed that their devices were operational and had faith that they would work, they did not execute all of the necessary steps in ensuring that their software was thoroughly tested or handled all edge cases. As a result of this, many patients who were relying on the Therac-25 died because of this causal factor. 

Inadequate use of Software Engineering Practices: This factor greatly ties into the other causal factors, but since the developers failed to properly make use of necessary software engineering practices during their development process, they were unable to detect the fatal flaws with their software, ultimately leading to the horrific Therac-25 accidents. For instance, the developers may not have performed a comprehensive review of their system, conducted thorough testing of the edge cases of its operationality, or did not fully document the requirements of the system, so they did not get implemented. There are many different possible points at which missteps during the development process can lead to catastrophic errors down the line. As such, failing to thoroughly review and document the development process can lead to the inadequate use of software engineering practices, which, in turn, can lead to a faulty final product, as was the case with Therac-25.

Inadequate Follow-Up Testing on Accident Reports: After the first instances of deaths due to the Therac-25 occurred, the developers of the technology did not take the time to investigate into those issues and determine whether the software was a cause. Overall there were at least hundreds of deaths of patients who used the Therac-25 software, however, even after the first few instances of system failure, an indication that there may be something wrong with its design or software, for instance, the development team didn't follow up promptly on the accident reports and did not conduct follow-up testing. As a result, this causal factor greatly contributed to the Therac-25 accidents. 

Lack of Defensive Design: During the development process of the Therac-25, the developers failed to utilize a defensive design architecture. Especially considering that they were working on such important software with high risks (patient's lives are at stake) and a low tolerance for errors (the deaths of their patients), it is a major issue that the developers of the Therac-25 did not properly utilize a defensive design for their software. If they had, then they may have been able to set up emergency protocols that would automatically shut the system down if it detects that the patient's condition is getting worse as a result of the technology. However, by failing to do so, they did not have the appropriate safety net that could have potentially caught some of the mistakes made during the earlier stages of development (such as the mistakes mentioned by the previous causal factors above).

20/20, all four factors are present and elaborated upon

Please define Social Contract Theory and explain some of the recent history and principles surrounding its development. Who is/was credited with this theory? What is/was their major contribution? What are some cases for and against Social Contract Theory? 

Your Answer:

The Social Contract Theory states that any rules or major decisions made by a society must be made via the implicit agreement of everyone in that society. Some of the surrounding principles include the Veil of Ignorance, which states that to prevent people from acting in their own self-interest, by being accepted into the society, all members are agreeing to the rules by which the society conducts themselves. Another associated principle is the Principle of Justice, which states that all members of the society must have the same basic rights and that if there are any inequities in the members' positions then everyone in the society must have an equal opportunity at obtaining those positions. Lastly, there is the Difference Principle, which states that if different people are treated different ways, then the most advantageous policies must be given to the most disadvantaged people. For instance, people are taxed different percentages of their salary in the US based on their income, with the more wealthy getting taxed a higher percent.

 

Some cases for the Social Contract Theory are that it explains a lot about our government's rules for instance and how people come to the agreement about laws and make decisions together about our societies rules.

Some cases against the Social Contract Theory are that since everyone can adhere to unspoken rules of the society they are a part of, many people may have not have explicitly agreed to those rules anywhere, so there is some ambiguity there.

9/10: Great definition and elaboration of the pros and cons for Social Contract Theory, as well as the Veil of Ignorance. No mention of who was credited with this theory (-1).

Howard Mart is a new department store that tracks its customers using facial recognition technology. When you first walk in, there's a sign on the door specifying that "facial recognition technology is being used to monitor for shoplifting." As it tracks you through the store, it logs which departments you're in and how long you spend there.  (E.g. it notices you went to the fishing section for 15 minutes but didn't pick up any items).  When you check out using your credit card, the system is able to match your face and name and other identifying information.  They are using this technology to send customers targeted special offers, texted to their cell phones automatically. 

    1. Discuss this practice from a consumer protection approach to privacy
    2. Discuss this from a free market approach to privacy.
    3. Would this be allowed in the European Union?  Explain why or why not and be specific.
Your Answer:

From a consumer protection approach to privacy, you should implement an opt in approach, where customers have the option to give their data away in collection if they want to. However, at the beginning, the sign at the front of the store only stated that there would be facial recognition software being used and did not make them aware of all the technologies being used. As such, they could not make an informed decision and when opting in they weren't aware at exactly what they were opting into. Also, they would be getting texting and promotional offers without agreeing to them for instance, when they only agreed to being monitored by facial detection algorithms.

From a free market approach to privacy, you should implement an opt out approach, where businesses can collect your information without your explicit consent and if the consumer does not want their data to be collected, then they can state this and opt out of the data collection. As such, there is not a valid argument for the business to use that data because they did not communicate what data they were exactly collecting to the user. However, if when they get the offers texted to them, if they can opt out of these services, that would be acceptable for instance.

This would not be allowed in the EU because of their GDPR standards they adhere to strict rules that protect the privacy of their members of society and they have transparent use of data policies. All members whose data is collected should be able to see how their data is being used. However, in this scenario more data is being collected, such as credit card information and other associated data when they only specified that facial detection was being used. As such, they consumer was not fully aware of all explicit softwares and data collection processes before entering the store and they were not able to make an informed decision when opting in.

4a: 10/10, good elaboration on consumer protection approach 4b: 10/10, good analysis on implementing an opt out approach 4c. 10/10
